LIF neurons emit discrete spikes that have no useful gradient with respect to their membrance potential. So people use surrogate gradients, but a [recent paper](https://direct.mit.edu/neco/article/37/5/886/128506) indicates that these don't neccessarily point in the right direction, which seems a bit inefficient to me. Let's consider a model with a nicer gradient, the Quadratic Integrate-and-Fire (QIF) neuron.

## QIF Dynamics
The QIFs membrane potential develops as follows:

\begin{equation}
\textcolor{ForestGreen}{\dot{U}} = \textcolor{ForestGreen}{U}(\textcolor{ForestGreen}{U} - 1) + \textcolor{purple}{I}
\end{equation}

If you look closely you see that the potential decays as long as $$\textcolor{ForestGreen}{U}$$ is less than or equal to 1. However, once it surpasses that, no more current is needed. The potential grows indefinitely. We see this below in Figure 1.

By changing the initial value of $$\textcolor{ForestGreen}{U}$$ you see that it behaves like a wet noodle until it suddenly reaches escape velocity.

And by the way, the current $$\textcolor{purple}{I}$$ is modeled as follows:

\begin{equation}
\textcolor{blue}{\tau_s} \textcolor{purple}{\dot{I}} = -\textcolor{purple}{I} + \textcolor{blue}{\tau_s} \sum_i \textcolor{red}{w_i} \sum_{t_i} \delta(t - t_i)
\end{equation}

where $$\textcolor{blue}{\tau_s}$$ is a synaptic time constant, the $$i$$ in the first sum loops through all pre-synaptic neurons, the weights $$\textcolor{red}{w_i}$$ are incoming synaptic connections, $$\delta$$ is the Dirac delta function, and $$t_i$$ are the spike times of the pre-synaptic neurons.

This is the change in incoming current. I think of it as being a decayed version of the previous current plus the addition of all the weighted spikes from pre-synaptic neurons.

## Spike Mechanism
The authors state that the QIF's membrane potential reaches infinity in finite time. This finite end time is defined as the spike time and it triggers a reset of the potential.

That's great, but how the heck do you take the derivative of that thing?

Well, the thing we differentiate here is the spike timing. We need to have exact target times and we can calculate the difference between the actual and target time.
