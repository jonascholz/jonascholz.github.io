LIF neurons emit discrete spikes that have no useful gradient with respect to their membrance potential. So people use surrogate gradients, but a recent paper {% include cite.html key="gygax2025" %} indicates that these don't neccessarily point in the right direction, which seems a bit inefficient to me. Let's consider a model with a nicer gradient, the Quadratic Integrate-and-Fire (QIF) neuron.

## QIF Dynamics
The QIFs membrane potential develops as follows:

\begin{equation}
\textcolor{ForestGreen}{\dot{U}} = \textcolor{ForestGreen}{U}(\textcolor{ForestGreen}{U} - 1) + \textcolor{purple}{I}
\end{equation}

If you look closely you see that the potential decays as long as $$\textcolor{ForestGreen}{U}$$ is less than or equal to 1. However, once it surpasses that, no more current is needed. The potential grows indefinitely. We see this below in Figure 1.

</figure>

By changing the initial value of $$\textcolor{ForestGreen}{U}$$ you see that it behaves like a wet noodle until it suddenly reaches escape velocity.

And by the way, the current $$\textcolor{purple}{I}$$ is modeled as follows:

\begin{equation}
\textcolor{blue}{\tau_s} \textcolor{purple}{\dot{I}} = -\textcolor{purple}{I} + \textcolor{blue}{\tau_s} \sum_i \textcolor{red}{w_i} \sum_{t_i} \delta(t - t_i)
\end{equation}

where $$\textcolor{blue}{\tau_s}$$ is a synaptic time constant, the $$i$$ in the first sum loops through all pre-synaptic neurons, the weights $$\textcolor{red}{w_i}$$ are incoming synaptic connections, $$\delta$$ is the Dirac delta function, and $$t_i$$ are the spike times of the pre-synaptic neurons.

This is the change in incoming current. It's a fancy continuous expression for decayed previous current plus all the weighted spikes from pre-synaptic neurons.

## Spike Time - A Different Derivative
The thing we differentiate here is the spike timing. We need to have exact target times and we can calculate the difference between the actual and target time. This is fundamentally different from rate coding, where we form an average over the spikes and differentiate with respect to each and every one.
