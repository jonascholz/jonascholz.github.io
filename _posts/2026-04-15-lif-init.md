Vanishing gradients are a problem. Losely speaking it's the problem that the final layer has some reasonable gradient but as it propagates to earlier layers it gets smaller. Mathematically backpropagation is just a chain of multiplications so if every layer has a small scaling factor then gradients will quickly become vanishingly small.

## Initial observations
Before we do anything, let's get a sense of what happens in a layer. We will study the distribution of LIF activations over time.

</div>

This is what happens with Aurora initialization {% include cite.html key="micheli2025" %} when the decay term is 0.96 (96% of state carries over to next timestep). We observe that the mean settles at some negative value over time. We can also observe that the variance grows a bit. Remarkably, the spike rate stays pretty stable throughout.

What if the decay is much faster, e.g. 0.6 (60% of state carries over)? Let's have a look:

<div style="text-align: center;">
  <img src="/assets/images/lif_membrane_over_time_06.gif" alt="LIF membrane potential distribution over time" style="max-width: 100%;">
</div>

If you passed by this distribution on the street, you might mistake it for a normal distribution. And it maintains its shape relatively well (albeit with a slightly longer left tail).

So this seems to indicate that the decay term flattens and stretches the distribution. This makes intuitive sense, because if the neuron maintains more of its state it is able to reach more extreme values. In turn there are more values on the edges instead of bunched up in the middle.

The lower the decay term, the closer we get to the expected normal distribution. This is shown in the next plot.

<img src="{{ '/assets/images/u_alpha_comparison.svg' | relative_url }}" alt="Membrane potential distribution for different alpha values" style="width: 50%; max-width: 700px; margin: 2em auto; display: block;">

And there's also some shift to the left because of the resets. When the reset happens, activations that surpass the threshold (> 0.5 in this case) get shifted back toward 0. This not only shifts the mean but also takes away some variance.

<img src="{{ '/assets/images/u_reset_comparison.svg' | relative_url }}" alt="Membrane potential distribution with and without reset" style="width: 50%; max-width: 700px; margin: 2em auto; display: block;">

While the reset doesn't seem as harmful it still costs us some variance. This would most definitely cause vanishing gradients if we were using conventional ANNs.

## What to do
My goal is to overcome vanishing gradients. In ANNs it pretty much comes down to maintaining variance 1 across membrane potentials of any given layer. In SNNs it's not that simple, as we will see. 

## Setup

The membrane potentials in a layer all follow the same distribution. It's therefore sufficient to consider a single neuron in this analysis.

$$
\cU{u_l[t]} = \calpha{\alpha} \cU{u_l[t-1]} + \cWin{W_l} \cX{X_{l-1}[t]} - \cS{x_l[t-1]} \ctheta{\theta}
$$

The membrane potential $\cU{u_l[t]}$ is a scalar value, the $\cWin{W_l}$ is a weight vector and $\cX{X_{l-1}[t]}$ and input vector. $\cS{x_l[t-1]}$ is the outgoing spike of this particular neuron at the previous time t-1.

Our goal is to find $\text{Var}[\cWin{W_l}]$ such that $\text{Var}[\cU{u}] = 1$ across layers and timesteps. 

## Splitting the variance

We take the variance of both sides:

$$
\text{Var}[\cU{u_l[t]}] = \text{Var}[\calpha{\alpha} \cU{u_l[t-1]} + \cWin{W_l} \cX{X_{l-1}[t]} - \cS{x_l[t-1]} \ctheta{\theta}]
$$

There's a neat way to rewrite variance when terms are independent. Specifically, it would be great to get $\cWin{W_l} \cX{X_{l-1}[t]}$ out. Certainly $\cWin{W_l}$ is sampled independently but the $\cX{X_{l-1}[t]}$ can be a bit more tricky. Let's assume its independence and see if we get away with it. NOTE this might be a shaky assumption.

$$
\text{Var}[\cU{u_l[t]}] = \underbrace{\text{Var}[\calpha{\alpha} \cU{u_l[t-1]} - \cS{x_l[t-1]} \ctheta{\theta}]}_{\text{reset-decay term}} + \underbrace{\text{Var}[\cWin{W_l} \cX{X_{l-1}[t]}]}_{\text{input term}}
$$

Micheli et al. {% include cite.html key="micheli2025" %} have already worked out the input term. The rewritten equation looks as follows, with the reset decay term still unsolved. In their paper they set it to 0.

$$
\text{Var}[\cU{u_l[t]}] = \underbrace{\text{Var}[\calpha{\alpha} \cU{u_l[t-1]} - \cS{x_l[t-1]} \ctheta{\theta}]}_{\text{reset-decay term}} + n_l\,\text{Var}[\cWin{W_l}] \cdot \cp{p}
\label{eq:var_u_input_expanded}
$$


## Expanding the reset-decay term

We expand the reset-decay term once again using the identity for any two random variables $A$ and $B$:

$$
\text{Var}[A - B] = \text{Var}[A] - 2\,\text{Cov}[A, B] + \text{Var}[B]
$$

And we find that:

$$
\underbrace{\text{Var}[\calpha{\alpha} \cU{u_l[t-1]} - \cS{x_l[t-1]} \ctheta{\theta}]}_{\text{reset-decay term}} = \text{Var}[\calpha{\alpha} \cU{u_l[t-1]}] - 2\,\text{Cov}[\calpha{\alpha} \cU{u_l[t-1]},\, \cS{x_l[t-1]} \ctheta{\theta}] + \text{Var}[\cS{x_l[t-1]} \ctheta{\theta}]
$$

Since $\calpha{\alpha}$ and $\ctheta{\theta}$ are deterministic constants (hyperparameters, not random variables), we can use $\text{Var}[cX] = c^2 \text{Var}[X]$ and $\text{Cov}[cX, dY] = cd\,\text{Cov}[X, Y]$:

$$
= \calpha{\alpha}^2 \text{Var}[\cU{u_l[t-1]}] - 2\calpha{\alpha}\ctheta{\theta}\,\text{Cov}[\cU{u_l[t-1]}, \cS{x_l[t-1]}] + \ctheta{\theta}^2 \underbrace{\text{Var}[\cS{x_l[t-1]}]}_{\text{variance of spike}}
$$

### Variance of a binary spike

Using the definition of variance, $\text{Var}[Y] = \mathbb{E}[Y^2] - \mathbb{E}[Y]^2$, we can write:

$$
\text{Var}[\cS{x_l[t-1]}] = \mathbb{E}[(\cS{x_l[t-1]})^2] - \mathbb{E}[\cS{x_l[t-1]}]^2
$$

$\cS{x_l[t-1]} \in \{0, 1\}$ is a binary spike, so $(\cS{x_l[t-1]})^2 = \cS{x_l[t-1]}$. Letting $\cp{p} = \mathbb{E}[\cS{x_l[t-1]}]$ denote the spike probability. We assume all layers fire at the same average rate $\cp{p}$, so this same value applies to the input spikes $\cX{X_{l-1}[t]}$ as well:

$$
\text{Var}[\cS{x_l[t-1]}] = \cp{p} - \cp{p}^2 = \cp{p}(1-\cp{p})
$$

Substituting back:

$$
\text{Var}[\calpha{\alpha} \cU{u_l[t-1]} - \cS{x_l[t-1]} \ctheta{\theta}] = \calpha{\alpha}^2 \text{Var}[\cU{u_l[t-1]}] - 2\calpha{\alpha}\ctheta{\theta}\,\text{Cov}[\cU{u_l[t-1]}, \cS{x_l[t-1]}] + \ctheta{\theta}^2 \cp{p}(1-\cp{p})
$$

So the full equation now reads:

$$
\text{Var}[\cU{u_l[t]}] = \calpha{\alpha}^2 \text{Var}[\cU{u_l[t-1]}] - 2\calpha{\alpha}\ctheta{\theta}\,\text{Cov}[\cU{u_l[t-1]}, \cS{x_l[t-1]}] + \ctheta{\theta}^2 \cp{p}(1-\cp{p}) + n_l\,\text{Var}[\cWin{W_l}] \cdot \cp{p}
$$

## Isolating the Weight variance

We seek a stationary variance, so we impose $\text{Var}[\cU{u_l[t]}] = \text{Var}[\cU{u_l[t-1]}] = 1$. Substituting:

$$
1 = \calpha{\alpha}^2 \cdot 1 - 2\calpha{\alpha}\ctheta{\theta}\,\text{Cov}[\cU{u_l[t-1]}, \cS{x_l[t-1]}] + \ctheta{\theta}^2 \cp{p}(1-\cp{p}) + n_l\,\text{Var}[\cWin{W_l}] \cdot \cp{p}
$$

Isolating the input term:

$$
n_l\,\text{Var}[\cWin{W_l}] \cdot \cp{p} = 1 - \calpha{\alpha}^2 + 2\calpha{\alpha}\ctheta{\theta}\,\text{Cov}[\cU{u_l[t-1]}, \cS{x_l[t-1]}] - \ctheta{\theta}^2 \cp{p}(1-\cp{p})
$$

Dividing by $n_l$ and $\cp{p}$.

\begin{equation}
\text{Var}[\cWin{W_l}] = \frac{1 - \calpha{\alpha}^2 - \ctheta{\theta}^2 \cp{p}(1-\cp{p}) + 2\calpha{\alpha}\ctheta{\theta}\,\text{Cov}[\cU{U}, \cS{x_l}]}{n_l\,\cp{p}}
\label{eq:var_w}
\end{equation}

## The expected value of U

Note that the mean of $\cU{U}$ is not zero. It will converge to its mean over time, but if we want it correct from step 1, we need to initialize it correctly. Thus, we need to find $\mathbb{E}[\cU{U}]$. By definition:

$$
\mathbb{E}[\cU{u_l[t]}] = \mathbb{E}\!\left[\calpha{\alpha} \cU{u_l[t-1]} + \cWin{W_l} \cX{X_{l-1}[t]} - \cS{x_l[t-1]} \ctheta{\theta}\right]
$$

We apply the same stationarity trick as before: we impose $\mathbb{E}[\cU{u_l[t]}] = \mathbb{E}[\cU{u_l[t-1]}] = \mathbb{E}[\cU{U}]$ and solve.

By linearity of expectation:

$$
\mathbb{E}[\cU{U}] = \calpha{\alpha}\,\mathbb{E}[\cU{U}] + \mathbb{E}[\cWin{W_l} \cX{X_{l-1}[t]}] - \ctheta{\theta}\,\mathbb{E}[\cS{x_l[t-1]}]
$$

Since $\cWin{W_l}$ and $\cX{X_{l-1}[t]}$ are independent (weights belong to layer $l$ and play no role in the dynamics of layer $l{-}1$), and $\mathbb{E}[\cWin{W_l}] = 0$:

$$
\mathbb{E}[\cWin{W_l} \cX{X_{l-1}[t]}] = \mathbb{E}[\cWin{W_l}]\,\mathbb{E}[\cX{X_{l-1}[t]}] = 0
$$

Substituting $\mathbb{E}[\cS{x_l[t-1]}] = \cp{p}$:

$$
\mathbb{E}[\cU{U}] = \calpha{\alpha}\,\mathbb{E}[\cU{U}] - \ctheta{\theta}\,\cp{p}
$$

Collecting the $\mathbb{E}[\cU{U}]$ terms on the left:

$$
\mathbb{E}[\cU{U}]\,(1 - \calpha{\alpha}) = -\ctheta{\theta}\,\cp{p}
$$

\begin{equation}
\mathbb{E}[\cU{U}] = \frac{-\ctheta{\theta}\,\cp{p}}{1 - \calpha{\alpha}}
\label{eq:mean_u}
\end{equation}

No distributional assumption is needed here as stationarity alone closes the equation.

## The thing with the spike rate
What is the spike rate for a given neuron? It's just the probability that the membrane potential is greater than the threshold.

If we know how the membrane potentials are distributed, we just have to take the area under curve from threshold onward.

The problem is that we don't know the distribution. To know the distribution, we have to know the weights, but that's the thing we were figuring out. It's a circular dependency where we need the spike rate to figure out the weights but we need the weights to figure out the spike rate.

Aurora init assumes the membrane potentials are normally distributed, which they are not and yet the estimate is remarkably close. 

<img src="{{ '/assets/images/spike_rates_per_layer.svg' | relative_url }}" style="width: 100%; max-width: 700px; margin: 2em auto; display: block;">

Maybe my understanding is off, but I think it should vastly overestimate the real spike rate. However, since the equation for the weight variance underestimates the reset-decay term, it seems to balance out by some miracle. 

## What the heck
Anyway, this goes beyond what I'm able to figure out within the next couple of weeks and my goal was stable variance. You can just fit an error term and the already decent estimate makes it so var(u)=1. I don't see a way to resolve the circular dependencies, unfortunately. Here's my final method:

1. Initialize $\text{Var}[\cWin{W_l}]$ as per \eqref{eq:var_w} and set the covariance term to 0
2. Initialize p as per the eq. 12 from Aurora init {% include cite.html key="micheli2025" %}
3. Use bisection to find a value for the covariance that has an average $\text{Var}[\cU{u}] = 1$ across some reasonable number of layers (~10)
4. Get $\mathbb{E}[\cU{U}]$ as per \eqref{eq:mean_u} and use it for initializing the $\cU{u}$ values and whenever you reset them
