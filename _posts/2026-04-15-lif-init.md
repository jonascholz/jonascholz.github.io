Vanishing gradients are a problem. Losely speaking it's the problem that the final layer has some reasonable gradient but as it propagates to earlier layers it gets smaller. Mathematically backpropagation is just a chain of multiplications so if every layer has a small scaling factor then gradients will quickly become vanishingly small.

## Initial observations
Before we do anything, let's get a sense of what happens in a layer. We will study the distribution of LIF activations over time.

</div>

This is what happens with Aurora initialization (TODO cite aurora) when the decay term is 0.96 (96% of state carries over to next timestep). We observe that the mean settles at some negative value over time. We can also observe that the variance grows a bit. Remarkably, the spike rate stays pretty stable throughout.

What if the decay is much faster, e.g. 0.6 (60% of state carries over)? Let's have a look:

<div style="text-align: center;">
  <img src="/assets/images/lif_membrane_over_time_06.gif" alt="LIF membrane potential distribution over time" style="max-width: 100%;">
</div>

If you passed by this distribution on the street, you might mistake it for a normal distribution. And it maintains its shape relatively well (albeit with a slightly longer left tail).

So this seems to indicate that the decay term flattens and stretches the distribution. This makes intuitive sense, because if the neuron maintains more of its state it is able to reach more extreme values. In turn there are more values on the edges instead of bunched up in the middle.

The lower the decay term, the closer we get to the expected normal distribution. This is shown in the next plot.

<img src="{{ '/assets/images/u_alpha_comparison.svg' | relative_url }}" alt="Membrane potential distribution for different alpha values" style="width: 50%; max-width: 700px; margin: 2em auto; display: block;">

And there's also some shift to the left because of the resets. When the reset happens, activations that surpass the threshold (> 0.5 in this case) get shifted back toward 0. This not only shifts the mean but also takes away some variance.

<img src="{{ '/assets/images/u_reset_comparison.svg' | relative_url }}" alt="Membrane potential distribution with and without reset" style="width: 50%; max-width: 700px; margin: 2em auto; display: block;">

While the reset doesn't seem as harmful it still costs us some variance. This would most definitely cause vanishing gradients if we were using conventional ANNs.

## What to do
My goal is to overcome vanishing gradients. In ANNs it pretty much comes down to maintaining variance 1 across membrane potentials of any given layer. In SNNs it's not that simple, as we will see. 

## Setup

All neurons in a layer have the properties. If we consider it a random variable then its membrane potential is a random variable. It follows the same distribution as all other neurons of that layer and has the same variance. This will make it conceptually easier, too! So single neuron going forward.

$$
\cU{u_l^t} = \calpha{\alpha} \cU{u_l^{t-1}} + \cWin{W_l} \cX{X_{l-1}^t} - \cS{x_l^{t-1}} \ctheta{\theta}
$$

So the $\cU{u_l^t}$ is a scalar value, the $\cWin{W_l}$ is a weight vector and $\cX{X_{l-1}^t}$ and input vector. $\cS{x_l^{t-1}}$ is the outgoing spike of this particular neuron at the previous time t-1.

Our goal is to find $\text{Var}[\cWin{W_l}]$ such that $\text{Var}[\cU{u}] = 1$ across layers and timesteps. 

## Splitting the variance

We take the variance of both sides:

$$
\text{Var}[\cU{U_l^t}] = \text{Var}[\calpha{\alpha} \cU{U_l^{t-1}} + \cWin{W_l} \cX{X_{l-1}^t} - \cS{X_l^{t-1}} \ctheta{\theta}]
$$

There's a neat way to rewrite variance when terms are independent. Specifically, it would be great to get $\cWin{W_l} \cX{X_{l-1}^t}$ out. 

In common initialization methods all weights are sampled independently. In other words $\cWin{W_l}$ from all the other terms. If this were true for $\cS{X_l^{t-1}}$ we could perform the nice rewrite. But let's go step by step.

$\cS{X_l^{t-1}}$ is independent of the constant $\calpha{\alpha}$. 

$$
\text{Var}[\cU{U_l^t}] = \underbrace{\text{Var}[\calpha{\alpha} \cU{U_l^{t-1}} - \cS{X_l^{t-1}} \ctheta{\theta}]}_{\text{reset-decay term}} + \underbrace{\text{Var}[\cWin{W_l} \cX{X_{l-1}^t}]}_{\text{input term}}
$$

## Expanding the input term

<summary>Skip it if you want</summary>

Since all neurons in a layer share the same weight statistics, we can study a single neuron. Its pre-activation is one row of $\cWin{W_l}$ dotted with the full input vector $\cX{X_{l-1}^t}$:

$$
\text{Var}[\cWin{W_l} \cX{X_{l-1}^t}] = \text{Var}\!\left[\sum_{j=1}^{n_l} \cWin{W_{l,j}} \cX{X_{l-1,j}^t}\right]
$$

where $\cWin{W_{l,j}}$ is the $j$-th weight in that row and $\cX{X_{l-1,j}^t}$ is the spike of neuron $j$ in layer $l{-}1$.

**Expand the variance of the sum.**

The general identity for the variance of a sum is:

$$
\text{Var}\!\left[\sum_j A_j\right] = \sum_j \text{Var}[A_j] + 2\sum_{j < k} \text{Cov}[A_j,\, A_k]
$$

Applied to our sum:

$$
\text{Var}\!\left[\sum_j \cWin{W_{l,j}} \cX{X_{l-1,j}^t}\right] = \sum_j \text{Var}[\cWin{W_{l,j}} \cX{X_{l-1,j}^t}] + 2\sum_{j < k} \underbrace{\text{Cov}[\cWin{W_{l,j}} \cX{X_{l-1,j}^t},\, \cWin{W_{l,k}} \cX{X_{l-1,k}^t}]}_{\text{cross-term}}
$$

We evaluate the cross-term for $j \neq k$:

$$
\text{Cov}[\cWin{W_{l,j}} \cX{X_{l-1,j}^t},\, \cWin{W_{l,k}} \cX{X_{l-1,k}^t}] = \underbrace{\mathbb{E}[\cWin{W_{l,j}} \cX{X_{l-1,j}^t} \cWin{W_{l,k}} \cX{X_{l-1,k}^t}]}_{\text{first term}} - \mathbb{E}[\cWin{W_{l,j}} \cX{X_{l-1,j}^t}]\,\mathbb{E}[\cWin{W_{l,k}} \cX{X_{l-1,k}^t}]
$$

We assume weights are i.i.d. across $j$ and independent of all input spikes (the latter holds at initialization since $\cWin{W_{l,j}}$ belongs to layer $l$ and plays no role in the dynamics of layer $l{-}1$). So $\cWin{W_{l,j}}$ is independent of $(\cWin{W_{l,k}},\, \cX{X_{l-1,j}^t},\, \cX{X_{l-1,k}^t})$ for $j \neq k$, giving:

$$
\mathbb{E}[\cWin{W_{l,j}} \cX{X_{l-1,j}^t} \cWin{W_{l,k}} \cX{X_{l-1,k}^t}] = \mathbb{E}[\cWin{W_{l,j}}] \cdot \mathbb{E}[\cX{X_{l-1,j}^t} \cWin{W_{l,k}} \cX{X_{l-1,k}^t}]
$$

We assume weights are zero-mean ($\mathbb{E}[\cWin{W_{l,j}}] = 0$), so both terms in the covariance are zero. Hence all cross-terms vanish, and the identity reduces to:

$$
\text{Var}\!\left[\sum_j \cWin{W_{l,j}} \cX{X_{l-1,j}^t}\right] = \sum_j \text{Var}[\cWin{W_{l,j}} \cX{X_{l-1,j}^t}]
$$

**Expand a single term.**

By definition of variance:

$$
\text{Var}[\cWin{W_{l,j}} \cX{X_{l-1,j}^t}] = \mathbb{E}\!\left[(\cWin{W_{l,j}})^2 (\cX{X_{l-1,j}^t})^2\right] - \mathbb{E}[\cWin{W_{l,j}} \cX{X_{l-1,j}^t}]^2
$$

Since $\cWin{W_{l,j}} \perp \cX{X_{l-1,j}^t}$, we apply $\mathbb{E}[f(X)g(Y)] = \mathbb{E}[f(X)]\,\mathbb{E}[g(Y)]$:

$$
= \mathbb{E}[\cWin{W_{l,j}}^2]\,\mathbb{E}[\cX{X_{l-1,j}^t}^2] - \mathbb{E}[\cWin{W_{l,j}}]^2\,\mathbb{E}[\cX{X_{l-1,j}^t}]^2
$$

Since $\mathbb{E}[\cWin{W_{l,j}}] = 0$, the second term vanishes:

$$
= \mathbb{E}[\cWin{W_{l,j}}^2]\,\mathbb{E}[\cX{X_{l-1,j}^t}^2]
$$

Since $\mathbb{E}[\cWin{W_{l,j}}] = 0$, the identity $\text{Var}[X] = \mathbb{E}[X^2] - \mathbb{E}[X]^2$ gives $\mathbb{E}[\cWin{W_{l,j}}^2] = \text{Var}[\cWin{W_{l,j}}]$. Since weights are i.i.d. across $j$, all share the same variance $\text{Var}[\cWin{W_l}]$:

$$
= \text{Var}[\cWin{W_l}]\,\mathbb{E}[\cX{X_{l-1,j}^t}^2]
$$

Since $\cX{X_{l-1,j}^t} \in \{0,1\}$ is binary, $(\cX{X_{l-1,j}^t})^2 = \cX{X_{l-1,j}^t}$, so $\mathbb{E}[\cX{X_{l-1,j}^t}^2] = \mathbb{E}[\cX{X_{l-1,j}^t}]$. By the uniform spike rate assumption, $\mathbb{E}[\cX{X_{l-1,j}^t}] = \cp{p}$ for all $j$:

$$
\text{Var}[\cWin{W_{l,j}} \cX{X_{l-1,j}^t}] = \text{Var}[\cWin{W_l}] \cdot \cp{p}
$$

**Sum over all $n_l$ terms.**

Since weights are i.i.d. and input spikes are identically distributed with mean $\cp{p}$, every term has the same variance $\text{Var}[\cWin{W_l}] \cdot \cp{p}$. Substituting back:

$$
\text{Var}[\cWin{W_l} \cX{X_{l-1}^t}] = n_l\,\text{Var}[\cWin{W_l}] \cdot \cp{p}
$$

$$
\text{Var}[\cU{U_l^t}] = \underbrace{\text{Var}[\calpha{\alpha} \cU{U_l^{t-1}} - \cS{X_l^{t-1}} \ctheta{\theta}]}_{\text{reset-decay term}} + n_l\,\text{Var}[\cWin{W_l}] \cdot \cp{p}
\label{eq:var_u_input_expanded}
$$


## Expanding the reset-decay term

We expand the left term using the identity for any two random variables $A$ and $B$:

$$
\text{Var}[A - B] = \text{Var}[A] - 2\,\text{Cov}[A, B] + \text{Var}[B]
$$

And we find that:

$$
\text{Var}[\calpha{\alpha} \cU{U_l^{t-1}} - \cS{X_l^{t-1}} \ctheta{\theta}] = \text{Var}[\calpha{\alpha} \cU{U_l^{t-1}}] - 2\,\text{Cov}[\calpha{\alpha} \cU{U_l^{t-1}},\, \cS{X_l^{t-1}} \ctheta{\theta}] + \text{Var}[\cS{X_l^{t-1}} \ctheta{\theta}]
$$

Since $\calpha{\alpha}$ and $\ctheta{\theta}$ are deterministic constants (hyperparameters, not random variables), we can use $\text{Var}[cX] = c^2 \text{Var}[X]$ and $\text{Cov}[cX, dY] = cd\,\text{Cov}[X, Y]$:

$$
= \calpha{\alpha}^2 \text{Var}[\cU{U_l^{t-1}}] - 2\calpha{\alpha}\ctheta{\theta}\,\text{Cov}[\cU{U_l^{t-1}}, \cS{X_l^{t-1}}] + \ctheta{\theta}^2 \text{Var}[\cS{X_l^{t-1}}]
$$

### Variance of a binary spike

Using the definition of variance, $\text{Var}[Y] = \mathbb{E}[Y^2] - \mathbb{E}[Y]^2$, we can write:

$$
\text{Var}[\cS{X_l^{t-1}}] = \mathbb{E}[(\cS{X_l^{t-1}})^2] - \mathbb{E}[\cS{X_l^{t-1}}]^2
$$

Now, $\cS{X_l^{t-1}} \in \{0, 1\}$ is a binary spike, so $(\cS{X_l^{t-1}})^2 = \cS{X_l^{t-1}}$. Letting $\cp{p} = \mathbb{E}[\cS{X_l^{t-1}}]$ denote the spike probability. We assume all layers fire at the same average rate $\cp{p}$, so this same value applies to the input spikes $\cX{X_{l-1}^t}$ as well:

$$
\text{Var}[\cS{X_l^{t-1}}] = \cp{p} - \cp{p}^2 = \cp{p}(1-\cp{p})
$$

Substituting back:

$$
\text{Var}[\calpha{\alpha} \cU{U_l^{t-1}} - \cS{X_l^{t-1}} \ctheta{\theta}] = \calpha{\alpha}^2 \text{Var}[\cU{U_l^{t-1}}] - 2\calpha{\alpha}\ctheta{\theta}\,\text{Cov}[\cU{U_l^{t-1}}, \cS{X_l^{t-1}}] + \ctheta{\theta}^2 \cp{p}(1-\cp{p})
$$

So the full equation now reads:

$$
\text{Var}[\cU{U_l^t}] = \calpha{\alpha}^2 \text{Var}[\cU{U_l^{t-1}}] - 2\calpha{\alpha}\ctheta{\theta}\,\text{Cov}[\cU{U_l^{t-1}}, \cS{X_l^{t-1}}] + \ctheta{\theta}^2 \cp{p}(1-\cp{p}) + \text{Var}[\cWin{W_l} \cX{X_{l-1}^t}]
$$

## Applying the fixed-point condition

We seek a stationary variance, so we impose $\text{Var}[\cU{U_l^t}] = \text{Var}[\cU{U_l^{t-1}}] = 1$. Substituting:

$$
1 = \calpha{\alpha}^2 \cdot 1 - 2\calpha{\alpha}\ctheta{\theta}\,\text{Cov}[\cU{U_l^{t-1}}, \cS{X_l^{t-1}}] + \ctheta{\theta}^2 \cp{p}(1-\cp{p}) + \text{Var}[\cWin{W_l} \cX{X_{l-1}^t}]
$$

Isolating the input term:

$$
\text{Var}[\cWin{W_l} \cX{X_{l-1}^t}] = 1 - \calpha{\alpha}^2 + 2\calpha{\alpha}\ctheta{\theta}\,\text{Cov}[\cU{U_l^{t-1}}, \cS{X_l^{t-1}}] - \ctheta{\theta}^2 \cp{p}(1-\cp{p})
$$

## Intermediate result (mostly correct I think)

Substituting back and solving for $\text{Var}[\cWin{W_l}]$:

$$
n_l\,\text{Var}[\cWin{W_l}] \cdot \cp{p} = 1 - \calpha{\alpha}^2 + 2\calpha{\alpha}\ctheta{\theta}\,\text{Cov}[\cU{U}, \cS{X_s}] - \ctheta{\theta}^2 \cp{p}(1-\cp{p})
$$

$$
\text{Var}[\cWin{W_l}] = \frac{1 - \calpha{\alpha}^2 - \ctheta{\theta}^2 \cp{p}(1-\cp{p}) + 2\calpha{\alpha}\ctheta{\theta}\,\text{Cov}[\cU{U}, \cS{X_s}]}{n_l\,\cp{p}}
$$

## The expected value of U

We want to find $\mathbb{E}[\cU{U}]$. By definition:

$$
\mathbb{E}[\cU{U_l^t}] = \mathbb{E}\!\left[\calpha{\alpha} \cU{U_l^{t-1}} + \cWin{W_l} \cX{X_{l-1}^t} - \cS{X_l^{t-1}} \ctheta{\theta}\right]
$$

We apply the same stationarity trick as before: we impose $\mathbb{E}[\cU{U_l^t}] = \mathbb{E}[\cU{U_l^{t-1}}] = \mathbb{E}[\cU{U}]$ and solve.

By linearity of expectation:

$$
\mathbb{E}[\cU{U}] = \calpha{\alpha}\,\mathbb{E}[\cU{U}] + \mathbb{E}[\cWin{W_l} \cX{X_{l-1}^t}] - \ctheta{\theta}\,\mathbb{E}[\cS{X_l^{t-1}}]
$$

Since $\cWin{W_l}$ and $\cX{X_{l-1}^t}$ are independent (weights belong to layer $l$ and play no role in the dynamics of layer $l{-}1$), and $\mathbb{E}[\cWin{W_l}] = 0$:

$$
\mathbb{E}[\cWin{W_l} \cX{X_{l-1}^t}] = \mathbb{E}[\cWin{W_l}]\,\mathbb{E}[\cX{X_{l-1}^t}] = 0
$$

Substituting $\mathbb{E}[\cS{X_l^{t-1}}] = \cp{p}$:

$$
\mathbb{E}[\cU{U}] = \calpha{\alpha}\,\mathbb{E}[\cU{U}] - \ctheta{\theta}\,\cp{p}
$$

Collecting the $\mathbb{E}[\cU{U}]$ terms on the left:

$$
\mathbb{E}[\cU{U}]\,(1 - \calpha{\alpha}) = -\ctheta{\theta}\,\cp{p}
$$

\begin{equation}
\mathbb{E}[\cU{U}] = \frac{-\ctheta{\theta}\,\cp{p}}{1 - \calpha{\alpha}}
\label{eq:mean_u}
\end{equation}

No distributional assumption is needed here -- stationarity alone closes the equation.

## The covariance term

The remaining unknown in our formula is $\text{Cov}[\cU{U}, \cS{X_s}]$. Since $\cS{X_s} = \mathbf{1}[\cU{U} > \ctheta{\theta}]$, this covariance is always non-negative: a higher membrane potential makes a spike more likely, so $\cU{U}$ and $\cS{X_s}$ are positively correlated. In terms of the formula, a positive covariance means spikes preferentially occur when $\cU{U}$ is large, so the reset actively damps variance -- the covariance term captures how much.

Resolving $\text{Cov}[\cU{U}, \cS{X_s}]$ requires evaluating $\mathbb{E}[\cU{U} \mid \cU{U} > \ctheta{\theta}]$, the truncated mean of $\cU{U}$ above threshold. This is a tail property of the stationary distribution of $\cU{U}$ and cannot be determined from its mean and variance alone -- it requires knowledge of the full distribution shape. We leave this as an open term for future work:

\begin{equation}
\text{Var}[\cWin{W_l}] = \frac{1 - \calpha{\alpha}^2 - \ctheta{\theta}^2 \cp{p}(1-\cp{p}) + 2\calpha{\alpha}\ctheta{\theta}\,\text{Cov}[\cU{U}, \cS{X_s}]}{n_l\,\cp{p}}
\label{eq:var_w_general}
\end{equation}

## Setting the covariance to zero

We set $\text{Cov}[\cU{U}, \cS{X_s}] = 0$. This is exact when $\calpha{\alpha} = 0$ (no leak, so the reset term drops out of the variance equation entirely) and is otherwise a conservative approximation: since the covariance is positive, setting it to zero underestimates the stabilizing effect of the spike reset, so we initialize with slightly smaller weights than optimal. The approximation improves as $\calpha{\alpha}$ decreases or as spikes become sparse (large $\ctheta{\theta}$, small $\cp{p}$).

The spike probability $\cp{p}$ is treated as a hyperparameter -- a target average spike rate chosen by the practitioner.

Substituting into \eqref{eq:var_w_general}:

\begin{equation}
\text{Var}[\cWin{W_l}] = \frac{1 - \calpha{\alpha}^2 - \ctheta{\theta}^2\,\cp{p}(1 - \cp{p})}{n_l\,\cp{p}}
\label{eq:var_w_final}
\end{equation}
